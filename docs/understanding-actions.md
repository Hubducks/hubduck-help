# How HubDuck thinks about actions

An email arrives about a Heritage Programme visit. It contains four things that need doing. One is yours. One belongs to the secretary. Two are for everyone. Without HubDuck, all four land on everyone's desk and nothing is clear.

HubDuck separates them.

## Not every action is for you

When HubDuck processes an email it asks: who actually needs to do this?

Some actions belong to one role. "Confirm the booking with the venue" — that's an admin task. Teachers don't need to see it, worry about it, or feel like they missed something.

Some actions belong to everyone. "Read and acknowledge the Child Protection policy" — that's for the whole school. Everyone sees it, everyone must tick it off.

Some actions are open for anyone to take. "Volunteer to supervise the visit" — no one is required to, but if you do, it's tracked.

HubDuck sorts this automatically. You only see what's yours.

## Three kinds of actions

| What you see | What it means | What you do |
|---|---|---|
| **For everyone** | Every person needs to complete this. The principal can see how many have. | Tick it when you've done it. |
| **One person handles this** | One person needs to own it. Once they mark it done, it's done for everyone. | If it's yours, mark it done. If it's not, you'll see who took it. |
| **Anyone can do this** | Open for whoever is available. Multiple people can contribute. | Claim it if you can help. |

## How does HubDuck know which is which?

Mostly it figures it out from context. A policy acknowledgment is always "for everyone." A booking confirmation is always "one person handles this." A volunteering request is always open.

Where it's ambiguous, it defaults to "for everyone" — because it's better to show something to more people than to hide it from someone who needed to see it.

Admins can always adjust the classification if the system got it wrong.

## What the principal sees

For every "for everyone" action the principal can see a live count. 12 of 15 teachers have acknowledged the Child Protection policy. 3 haven't. That's the audit trail — without anyone having to chase individually.

Teachers can flag an action as something parents or guardians need to do — consent forms, attendance confirmation, instrument reminders. When the parent surface launches, these will appear directly in the parent's view. Same model, different audience.
